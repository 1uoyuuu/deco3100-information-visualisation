In a world teeming with diverse species, our relentless pursuit of resources such as land, water, food, and shelter holds profound sway over our surroundings. Yet, the impact of our relentless quest extends well beyond our own kind, leaving a trail of consequences that shape the lives of other creatures and disrupt the delicate equilibrium of the natural world.

Throughout the ages, humans have been actively shaping ecosystems through a combination of direct competition and hunting of other animals, as well as indirect means such as deforestation and modifications in land use for agriculture.

Do we humans have the right to manipulate the natural world for our own benefit?

If we believe that the Earth belongs solely to humans, then it may seem reasonable to assume that we have an unrestricted right to exploit its resources.

But the question is: is it fair to assume that the Earth belongs solely to humans?

To understand our place in the planet's ecosystem, we can measure our portion in terms of biomass, which refers to the total mass of living organisms in an ecosystem. Biomass is measured in tonnes of carbon, as carbon is a key building block of life (Bar-On et al., 2018).

Global Biomass Distribution

Take a look at the global biomass distribution chart on the left, and see if you can locate the position of humanity amidst the rich diversity of life forms that inhabit our planet.

As we delve into the fascinating tapestry of life on Earth, it becomes strikingly evident that the vast array of animal species comprises a mere fraction, constituting less than 1% of the total biomass. And within this animalia kingdom, humanity's role is but a humble thread, representing a mere 2% of the overall animal biomass. Astonishingly, this means that humanity presence amounts to a mere droplet in the vast ocean of Earth's biomass, accounting for less than 0.01% of the entire biomass system.

0.01%, that's our actual place in the planet we claimed to be our own home.

Hey, let's not forget about all the livestocks we raise.

Although we may seem small in terms of our own biomass on this planet, it is crucial to recognise the significant impact of the livestock we raise to fulfill our dietary needs. Surprisingly, the combined population of livestock under human care contributes a substantial 4% to the total animal biomass, surpassing the combined biomass of our own species by almost double.

Indeed, this astounding figure underscores the remarkable impact of livestock on the intricate balance of biomass in our world, serving as a vivid reminder of the inseparable connection between humans and the animals we raise for sustenance.

Despite our tiny biomass, the impact of our actions on the planet is substantial.

Our intelligence has granted us the extraordinary power to shape the world around us, positioning us as the most dominant species on Earth. However, with great power comes great responsibility. It is crucial for us to reflect on whether we have wielded this power wisely and considerately.

Land Use Change Since 10000BCE

It is clear that humanity has not lived up to its responsibilities, at least for now.

Throughout history, humanity has conquered hunger through the innovation of agriculture and the domestication of animals as vital food sources. However, this advancement has come at a price, particularly in terms of land use. In our pursuit to satiate our ever-expanding appetites and ensure our survival, we have significantly altered the Earth's landscapes by clearing wild areas to make way for crop cultivation and livestock rearing.

Until now, we have utilised nearly half of the habitable land on Earth for agriculture, and it has created much impact on the environment we can ever imagined.

What have we done?

  • Deforestation

    We have lost one-third of forests since the beginning of human agriculture.

  • Greenhouse Gas Emission

    Food production alone accounts for one-quarter of of total greenhouse gas emission.

  • Wildlife Habitat Loss

    Thousands of wildlife species are at the risk of losing their habitats.

What have we gained?

Our dietary lives are getting better.

While it is important to acknowledge that there are still some populations suffering from famine, on average, the global trend reveals a continuous increase in food consumption. Since the 1960s, the average daily energy supply per capita has steadily risen and is now approaching 3000 kcal per day. Furthermore, the consumption of animal products has nearly doubled during this period.

Average Daily Energy Supply Per Capita

Wildlife's lives are getting worse.

The Living Planet Index (LPI) serves as a measure of the average change in animal population numbers worldwide. Alarmingly, the populations of the animals studied have declined by 70% in the last fifty years. While this data may not capture the entire spectrum of animal trends globally, it serves as a wake-up call for humanity to take notice of the impact we have on the world's wildlife (Ritchie, 2022).

According to the FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization), with current agriculture expansion, global meat consumption is projected to increase by 50% by 2050, reaching a total of 557 million tonnes.

While definitive answers may elude us, one undeniable truth remains: Wildlife cannot bear the consequences. Thousands of species face the imminent threat of habitat loss, with some hovering on the edge of extinction by 2050.

What if sacrificing wildlife for human benefits ultimately jeopardizes our own future?

Our insatiable appetites are cooking up the planet.

While significant efforts are directed towards addressing climate change through renewable energy and energy efficiency, it is imperative not to overlook the critical role of our food system. The undeniable reality is that our insatiable appetites, propelling relentless growth, have the capacity to make a substantial contribution to a 2 °C rise in global warming solely through emissions from our food system.

By 2100, the projected total emissions from food system alone will reach 1356 billion tonnes (Clark et al., 2020).

Global Emission Budget to Stay Below 2°C Target with Projected Food Emissions by 2100

Human survival hangs in the balance.

As global temperatures continue to rise, extreme temperatures are expected to increase across the world. This means that virtually all regions inhabited by humans will face the impacts of some of the warmest extreme temperatures resulting from climate change (NASA, 2019).

With just 1.5 °C global warming, up to half as many people around the planet may experience water stress. The degree will vary from region to region. People in river basins, especially in the Middle and Near East, will be particularly vulnerable (NASA, 2019).

The occurrence of deadly heatwaves, similar to the ones experienced in 2015 by India and Pakistan, could become an annual occurrence at 2 °C warming (NASA, 2019).

Certain areas like Eastern North America will experience a rise in heavy rainfall events, potentially leading to increased occurrences of high flooding events (NASA, 2019).

There is no planet B.

We are at a critical juncture where we must right the wrongs of the past and embark on a transformative journey towards a better future before it is too late. Undoubtedly, considerable progress has been made on a larger scale to combat climate change through initiatives such as renewable energy and sustainable industry. However, it is understandable that these efforts may sometimes feel disconnected from our daily lives.

So what can we do as individuals to make a tangible difference in the fight against climate change? The answer is simple: we just need a slight tweak of our diet plan.

It turns out, our insatiable appetite for meat stands as the ultimate culprit behind current intensive agriculture expansion.

Raising livestocks require far more land than growing crops. This is especially true for beef and lamb, it takes around 100 times as much land to produce 1000 kilocalorie of beef or lamb versus any kind of plant-based alternatives.

Top 10 Land Intensive Food per 1000 Kilocalories

Less meat means more land free.

Reducing meat consumption reduces the demand for agricultural land. A global shift towards a plant-based diet could save approximately 3 billion hectares, which is nearly four times the size of Australia. While transitioning to a complete vegan lifestyle may appear daunting, even small steps can still make a substantial difference.

Global Land Use for Agriculture across Different Diets

Adjusting our diets means more than that.

Remember the emission budget we have? Embracing healthy or plant-rich diets (reduced meat, not vegan) holds immense potential for reducing greenhouse gas emissions originating from food production, which is crucial if we want to limit global warming to within 2°C.
